The Rest of the Story — Textron & Tamarack Diverge

Prospects will eventually ask harder questions: "Why doesn't Textron support you anymore?" or "What happened?" You need to answer with confidence — not defensiveness — and pivot to why Tamarack is stronger for it.

🔑 Frame This Correctly
This is not a victim story. It's a story about a startup that built something so good that even when a Fortune 500 OEM walked away, the product's engineering and the market's demand carried it forward. That's the strongest endorsement there is.

What Happened

1

The Installation Economics Gap

Tamarack's Transformation Centers complete a SMARTWING installation in approximately 250 hours, including paint. Textron's service centers — even after performing over 30 installations — required 750–1,000 hours per aircraft. At that labor rate, they were losing money on every install. When the economics didn't work for them, they began looking for a reason to step back.

2

November 2018 — The Catalyst

An accident involving a SMARTWING-equipped CJ in Indiana (aircraft was in compliance with all service bulletins) triggered an NTSB investigation. Separately, CJ3 fleet performance data created questions about whether certain aircraft were meeting expected cruise numbers. These events gave Textron the opening to distance themselves from the program.

3

2019 — Textron Walks Away

Textron Aviation stopped supporting Tamarack-modified aircraft — including 29 of the roughly 200 installations that Textron's own service centers had performed. The Product Support Agreement effectively ended. Tamarack became, in Textron's words, persona non grata.

4

The Grounding & the Comeback

EASA issued an emergency AD grounding the fleet in April 2019. The FAA followed under political pressure. Within 77 days, EASA lifted the grounding — citing existing service bulletins as sufficient. The FAA followed within days. By July 22, 2019, Tamarack resumed installations. The NTSB's final report (November 2022) found no design deficiency, and in February 2024, the NTSB granted Tamarack's petition for reconsideration — a rare reversal.

5

CJP — A Missed Opportunity for Advocacy

The Citation Jet Pilots Owner Association (CJP), which had financial ties to Textron, did not advocate for the SMARTWING fleet during the grounding or the NTSB process — even though hundreds of their own members were affected. This was a reminder that industry organizations sometimes have conflicting priorities.

6

Tamarack's Strategic Pivot

Rather than remain dependent on any single OEM relationship, Tamarack diversified: military contracts, airline programs, and expanding the Transformation Center network. The company emerged leaner, more self-sufficient, and with a product that had been publicly vindicated by both EASA and the NTSB.

7

2026 — Reconnecting with Textron

Tamarack has since earned certified improvements on the M2 platform. A meeting with Textron is scheduled for May 2026 to explore re-engagement. The engineering case is stronger than ever, the fleet has continued to grow, and the M2 improvements represent a clear value proposition for Textron's customer base.

Certification & Testing — What It Takes to Earn an STC

When a prospect asks "How do I know this is safe?" — you need to know exactly what Tamarack went through to earn this approval. This isn't hand-waving. This is years of engineering, testing, and regulatory scrutiny.

🔑 Why This Matters in Sales
Most prospects have no idea what goes into an STC. When you can walk them through the rigor — structural tests, flutter dives, icing flights, EMI testing, a full year of DO-160 lab work — you're not just selling a winglet. You're selling engineering credibility that exceeds what many OEM original certifications require.

The STC Standard

A Supplemental Type Certificate (STC) is the only legal way to sell aftermarket modifications for certified aircraft. The certification standards must meet or exceed those of the original aircraft certification. Tamarack's eco-SMARTWING was developed in collaboration with EASA, Cessna, Textron, and the FAA.

Design & Analysis

  • Estimated forces on the entire aircraft — wing, tail, fuselage, extension, winglet — across thousands of load cases
  • Variables: aircraft weights, fuel loads, speeds, altitudes, turbulence, gusts, high-G maneuvers
  • System failure analysis using Part 25 (airline) standards — because Part 23 had no regulations for this type of system
  • Failure scenarios: TACS full deflection (hard-over) at maximum speed, TACS jammed, oscillation analysis
  • Iterative design loop: multiple prototype variations analyzed, tested, and refined until all regulations were met

Carbon Fiber — Extra Rigor

  • Load Enhancement Factor (LEF): Carbon fiber loses up to 50% of strength when hot. Regulators require an additional 147% factor on top of normal requirements
  • Barely Visible Impact Damage (BVID): Before testing, carbon parts were struck with an 8 lb metal rod dropped from 3.11 feet at worst-case locations — simulating real-world damage a pilot might not see on preflight
  • Parts then had to pass limit and ultimate load testing with the impact damage and thermal factor applied

Flutter Testing

  • Ground Vibration Tests (GVT): Aircraft covered in accelerometers and deflection sensors, shaken in multiple modes to find any tendency for vibrations to grow
  • Flight testing: Strict "build-up" approach — speed increased by only 20 knots per flight while shaking the aircraft with TACS to try to induce flutter
  • Grand finale: CJ3 dived from FL450 at M.785, near full throttle, both pilots pushing forward, while vibrating the wing with TACS
  • Result: Aircraft proven flutter-free to 1.4 × MMO (40% faster than maximum operating speed)
💬 Sales Talking Point
"We dived a CJ3 from FL450 at Mach .785 while deliberately trying to shake the wings apart. The airplane was perfectly stable. Flutter-free to 40% above max operating speed."

Flight Characteristics & Failure Testing

CJ3 Fleet Performance Variance — Know the Nuance

⚠️ Internal Knowledge Only. This is for your understanding so you can have intelligent conversations with CJ3 owners. Do not volunteer this information — use it only when a prospect raises performance concerns.

What Happened

N52ET (the demo aircraft flown by many CJ3 pilots and most early customers) was performing exceptionally — 700 pph at or very close to MMO was the norm. Multiple transatlantic flights and a circumnavigation were completed. However, some CJ3 customers reported different results than expected.

The Technical Explanation

N52ET, while in tolerance, shows a 2% variance (8 KTAS / .016M) from true MMO. Near MMO, drag rises sharply and nonlinearly, so even a small airspeed difference has a major effect on fuel flow. Some fleet aircraft are on the other side of the tolerance band.

Key physics to understand (if the prospect goes deep):

  • Parasitic drag comes from wetted area, intersections, antennae, and control system coves. Winglets add a very small amount.
  • Induced drag comes from lifting surfaces — winglets dramatically reduce it by increasing effective aspect ratio (30% reduction in induced drag coefficient).
  • Wave (Mach) drag rises sharply near transonic speeds. This is where the fleet variance appears — small manufacturing tolerance differences create noticeable performance divergence near MMO.

Demo Flights & Proposals Wk 3

Sessions 16–17 — The demo flight is the most powerful tool in the sales process. Most deposits are placed around FL250 based on sustained climb rate alone.

Source: Nick Guida, Founder & CEO — “Proper Demo Flight with Tips & Tricks for CJ”

1. Flight Planning — Set the Stage

Before anyone gets near the aircraft, walk the prospect through the numbers on paper. This is where you plant the seeds of amazement.

  • WAT Tables (Weight/Altitude/Temperature): Show baseline vs. ATLAS side-by-side. At 5,000′ / 44°C the modified aircraft allows 549 lb more takeoff weight (Flaps Up). At 9,000′ / 25°C the advantage is 490 lb Flaps Up and 1,042 lb Flaps 15. Many pilots don’t run these numbers — reintroduce the concept.
  • Climb Gradient (OEI): Tamarack certified increased One Engine Inoperative climb gradients. “If it’s this good at extreme conditions, imagine the everyday safety margin increase.”
  • Payload-Endurance: The 400 lb MZFW increase creates 2 additional available seats on short flights. At 1,000 lb payload the Tamarack line shows ~3.5 hours endurance with 600 lb reserve — far beyond baseline.
  • Fuel Planning: For a 3.5-hour flight expect ~94 gph total (engine start to shutdown). Baseline data above 3 hours is scarce; above 4 hours it barely exists. “Tamarack set the unofficial world record for a straight CJ — 1,853 nm with a 26-kt average tailwind, 6:16 duration.”
  • Temperature at cruise: “For the Tamarack CJ, OAT doesn’t matter. The aircraft will climb and accelerate even in ISA+15.”

If this is a demo-only flight, plan a big triangle or square with 100 nm legs. File with remarks: ROUND ROBIN FLIGHT DEMO ANY HEADING FOR UNRESTRICTED CLIMB TO FL450 THEN 20 MINUTES AT FL450 THEN RTB. Load the aircraft as heavy as possible for the conditions.

2. Preflight — The Walk-Around

  • “Notice how good your new airplane looks as you walk toward it.” The aesthetic aspect is a huge selling point.
  • Show the winglet details: hinges, cotter pins, glareshield, lights, screws, de-ice vent.
  • Check the TACS for freedom of movement and return to neutral.
  • Discuss the AFMS checklist — the new checklist additions are minimal: “Slow down if there is an INOP light.”

3. Startup — BIT Test & Seed Planting

  • After avionics on, do the BIT (Built-In Test): push the ATLAS button 3 times within 3 seconds. Watch the two rows of annunciator lights (top then bottom then both), then observe a winglet as the TACS does its position check.
  • Note the air temperature. Pull out the CesNav or planning book and show: at this weight and temp, what the high/hot takeoff weight would be baseline vs. modified.
  • Plant the seed: “Show that the climb to FL410 baseline would require a step or two and a couple of hours. Even a CJ3 at ISA+15 is a 4-hour climb! We’ll be there in 30 minutes.”

4. Taxi

  • Notice ATC asking questions about the aircraft and how good it looks on the ramp.
  • Pay attention to the extra 2.5 ft per side while taxiing.
  • When getting clearance, remind that “any heading will work for an uninterrupted climb.”

5. Takeoff & Climb — The Money Shot

  • Takeoff: “Notice the aircraft jump off the ground.” If the pilot is comfortable, have them load up the stick during ground roll to feel aircraft lightness. “It feels as if rotation could be at a lower speed.”
  • Once airborne, notice the acceleration. Ask the pilot to hand-fly without yaw damper and A/P. “They all reach immediately for autopilot — show them how easy the aircraft is to fly now without it.”
  • Climb profile (by model):
ModelPitch AngleNotes

  • 3,000–4,000 fpm initial climb rate. Notice sustained high climb rate with only slight airspeed reduction.
  • Ask ATC for heading changes in the mid-to-high thirties — “It is impressive to be climbing and turning in the high thirties.”
  • Do turns during the climb to get the TACS to deploy. If bumpy, watch the TACS do their magic.
  • “Most of the deposits or commitments are made around FL250 based on sustained climb rate.”

6. Level Off & Cruise — Let the Aircraft Speak

  • Time to FL410: ~30 minutes, give or take a couple for temperature and technique variations.
  • Acceleration after level off: ~1 knot per 3–5 seconds. 9–12 minutes until AOA index is down and airspeed is 165–170 KIAS.
  • “Less than 600 lb used to FL410, heavy and hot? Talk about that.”
  • Show remaining fuel: ~2,800–2,900 lb on board. “Where could you go with that much fuel? 4 more hours remain!”
  • Disconnect autopilot at ~170 KIAS — feel the stability. Kick the rudder and watch it return within a few cycles. “Much different than stock.”
  • Hand-fly and do heading changes at FL410 — comfortable and predictable.
  • Burning ~600 pph at cruise. “The yaw damper INOP limitation is lifted for M2.”

7. Descent & Approach

  • Notice the slightly reduced N1 required and the stability.
  • At 15,000′, perform slow flight and stalls: rock-solid feel, quick recovery.
  • Experiment with “single engine” climb at idle — look up V2 for the weight and note the amazing climb rate. (Only if everyone is comfortable.)
  • In the pattern: ~7–8% reduction in N1 to maintain normal approach descent rates.
  • “The handling qualities were excellent. It trimmed up beautifully, virtually hands-off.”

8. Landing & Shutdown

  • Using published Vref, landings visually seem a bit fast due to lower deck angle. Pilots can use 0.6 AOA for a speed reduction.
  • “Only a very slight flare is required, and you can hit the spot you aim for. The plane does not float if flown to AFM procedures.”
  • Fuel burned: Typically 25–30% less than the pilot would have used for that length of flight. Do the math on the spot.
  • “Less fuel could be traded for even better performance — tankering fuel is now a real possibility to remote places or expensive ones.”

  • “How does that compare to what you’re used to?”
  • “How could this help with your own mission?”

Bridge to Proposal

  • Bridge to specific ROI numbers for their aircraft

The Close — Mastering the Ask Wk 3

Session 18 — Closing isn’t one moment at the end. It’s a series of micro-commitments throughout the entire sales process. If you did your job in discovery, the close is just a formality.

Cardone Rule #1: The sale is not made when the customer says yes. The sale is made when the money is collected, the PSA is signed, and the slot is booked. Everything before that is still selling.

1. Closing Mindset — Before You Open Your Mouth

  • You are doing them a favor. SMARTWING™ makes their aircraft safer, more capable, and worth more money. Not asking for the sale is doing them a disservice.
  • Every “no” is just “not yet.” The average sale takes 5 follow-ups. Most reps quit after 2.
  • Price is never the real objection. If they’re talking about price, they’re interested. Nobody haggles on something they don’t want.
  • Be unreasonable about follow-up. The prospect is busy flying their airplane. They’re not thinking about you. That’s your job.
  • Stay in the deal. Don’t let silence kill it. If they go quiet, it means you haven’t given them a reason to respond, not that they said no.

3. The 10 Closes — Have All of Them Ready

You don’t pick one. You use the right one for the moment. If the first doesn’t land, transition to the next. A real closer has 3–4 loaded at all times.

① The Assumptive Close

Skip “would you like to” entirely. Act as if the decision is made.

“Let’s look at the installation calendar. What quarter works best for your flying schedule?”

When: Strong buying signals. They’ve done a demo flight. They’re asking logistics questions.

② The “If I Could, Would You?” Close

Isolate the objection. Confirm that solving it closes the deal.

“If I could get you into the June slot at Sandpoint, would you be ready to move forward?”

“If I could show you the financing breaks it down to $3K/month, would that change things?”

When: They have one specific concern. Solve it conditionally, then solve it for real.

③ The Summary Close

Stack every benefit they agreed to during discovery, then ask.

“So we’ve got the range to make your NYC–FLL trip nonstop. The Vref data shows 100% value recovery. You loved the climb rate on the demo. The install fits in your September downtime. What else do you need to see?”

When: You’ve had multiple touchpoints and they’ve validated the benefits along the way.

④ Reduce to the Ridiculous

Break the price down until it sounds small. Compare it to what they already spend.

“$255K over a 5-year hold is $51K a year. That’s $4,250 a month — less than your monthly fuel bill. And Vref gives you 100% of it back at resale. You’re not spending money, you’re moving it.”

When: Price objection. Always anchor against costs they’re already paying (fuel, insurance, hangar).

⑤ The Takeaway Close

Pull back slightly. Create scarcity. Make them chase.

“I only have two Q3 slots left and I’ve got another owner looking at July. I don’t want to pressure you, but I also don’t want you to miss the window.”

“Honestly, the pre-pay incentive expires at month-end. After that, same mod, just more expensive. Your call.”

When: Stalled deals. Prospects who “need to think about it.” Only use if scarcity is real.

⑥ The Puppy Dog Close

Let the product sell itself. Get them in the aircraft.

“Let’s set up a demo flight. No commitment. I just want you to feel the difference at FL410. After that, you tell me.”

When: Skeptics. Engineers. “Show me, don’t tell me” types. If they fly it, they buy it — most deposits happen at FL250 on the climb.

⑦ The Ben Franklin Close

Put it on paper. Pros on the left, cons on the right. Let math win.

“Let’s just lay it out. On this side: up to 33% range increase, 400 lb MZFW increase, Vref value, nonstop range, safety margin, ramp appeal — plus you burn meaningfully less fuel on every leg. On this side: 10 days downtime and the check. Which side wins?”

When: Analytical buyers. The pilot who needs to justify it to a partner or board.

⑧ The “What’s It Costing You to Wait?” Close

Quantify inaction. Make doing nothing feel expensive.

“You fly about 300 hours a year. With Tamarack you eliminate the fuel stop on your long legs and burn meaningfully less on every other flight — we’ll model your top-3 routes in the Mission Analysis simulator and show you the actual annual number. Plus your aircraft is worth less every month without it on Vref. Every quarter you wait costs you real money.”

When: The “I’m not in a hurry” prospect. They need to feel the cost of delay.

⑨ The Third-Party Close

Get a current customer to close for you. Social proof is stronger than any pitch.

“Would it help to talk to Dennis Eiler? He’s on his second install — CJ and CJ2. I can connect you today.”

When: Trust gap. AD/Chapter 11 concern. “My mechanic says no.” Let a peer do the heavy lifting.

⑩ The Sharp Angle Close

When they ask for a concession, use it to close immediately.

Them: “Can you do it during my annual in October?”
You: “If I can make October work, can we sign the PSA today?”

Them: “What about the pre-pay discount?”

When: They ask for something specific. Every request is a close opportunity.

Subject: 33% Range increase vs. fuel savings claim

Dear valued team members,

I need to make this point once and for all. From this point forward I want to change all our marketing messaging from "up to 33% fuel savings" to "up to 33% range increase".

"Up to 33% fuel savings" is misleading (not misleading in an intentional malicious way) and inaccurate. My experience for the past 10 years is that "up to 33% fuel savings" creates confusion, suspicion, and just feels wrong to me. I am tired of having this conversation, so I am putting this to bed here and now.

This is not an engineer talking, this is a pilot and operator of these sophisticated, awesome machines. I do not buy the argument that "up to 33% fuel savings" is puffery, thus acceptable. We have hard numbers that we can use and it's just as good.

The crisp and irrefutable statement is "up to 33% range increase", and it cannot be misunderstood. Fuel savings is expected each flight, and that will happen, but max range is expected occasionally, when you want to go someplace far.

The 33% number comes from the fact that a Tamarack CJ can conservatively fly for an hour longer than a flatwing CJ. 4 hours / 3 hours − 1 = 33%. That is how simple this is, and it should be applied to the range, not fuel savings, because all the fuel in the tank is burned at that point of max range, and you are not "saving" anything.

But, in reality, the "fuel savings" is much greater than 33%!!! (as I show later in this diatribe)

Please follow the example below, which is the most basic way, and one of the most accurate ways trip planning can be done.

Flatwing CJ — fuel-burn planning (must step climb to go far)

  • Max fuel: 3,400 lb
