Textron & Tamarack — The Relationship

Understanding this history is essential. Prospects will ask — and your answer needs to radiate confidence, not caution. Here's why you can.

🔑 The Bottom Line for Sales
Tamarack didn't reverse-engineer this product in a garage. Cessna (now Textron Aviation) opened their entire engineering vault — structural drawings, aero models, flight test data, fatigue analysis, flutter models, electrical schematics — and worked side-by-side with Tamarack for 5+ years across hundreds of joint engineering sessions. The SMARTWING was developed with the OEM, not despite them.

The Partnership Timeline

1

November 2012 — MOU Signed

Cessna and Tamarack Aerospace Group sign a formal Memorandum of Understanding, launching the joint development program. Tamarack submits a comprehensive OEM Data Request covering structures, flight test, electrical systems, EMI/HIRF/lightning, and systems safety. Cessna begins providing wing tip drawings, CAD models, dynamic FE models, flutter reports, wing loads, fatigue analysis, and engine performance data.

2

Dec 2012 – Mar 2013 — Deep Engineering Begins

Formal Engineering Change Memos (ECMs) flow between teams: oscillation studies, fatigue analysis methods (7 load cases for baseline and ATLAS-modified configurations), DO-160G qualification plans, gust analysis, flutter analysis with ice accretion effects. Cessna's top engineers — structures, aero, flutter, icing, systems — are directly engaged. This is not arm's-length; this is shared engineering.

3

2013 — Icing, Performance & Certification Planning

Joint teleconferences on icing analysis (ice shapes, wind tunnel data from Cox facility, runback characterization), flutter vibration with ice accretion, stall warning, and buffet flight test planning. Cessna provides flight test recording procedures and performance methodology. Topics discussed: static margins, gust analysis spectrum, DO-160G qualification conditions for higher-altitude CJ2/CJ3 operations.

4

October 2013 — Formal Agreement Signed

The relationship deepens with a formal Cessna Agreement (including Exhibit C), superseding the original MOU. This creates a binding framework for continued collaboration, data sharing, and commercialization support.

5

2014 — Joint Flight Test & Performance Validation

Intensive joint performance work: W/δ cruise data collection at multiple altitudes (19K, 25K, 35K, 45K ft), sawtooth climb tests (FL220 through FL400), baseline-vs-modified configuration comparisons on N86LA (CJ SN 525-0012). Cessna's aero engineers validate TAG's flight test data against their own performance models. Results: 6% cruise improvement (both plot and chart methods), fuel flow improvement ranging from 4% at high N1 to >15% at low N1. In-person meetings at Cessna headquarters in Wichita. Key topic: guaranteed performance data ready for NBAA 2014 deposits.

Joint sessions on ATLAS Control Unit positioning inside the CJ fuselage — evaluating under-seat, below-floorboard, and behind-seat locations. This level of detail — "How hot does the box get?" — shows how embedded Tamarack was inside Cessna's engineering process.

Cessna and Tamarack sign a Product Support Agreement, formalizing the post-certification support relationship. Installation work channeled through Cessna-owned service centers.

The Rest of the Story — Textron & Tamarack Diverge

Prospects will eventually ask harder questions: "Why doesn't Textron support you anymore?" or "What happened?" You need to answer with confidence — not defensiveness — and pivot to why Tamarack is stronger for it.

🔑 Frame This Correctly
This is not a victim story. It's a story about a startup that built something so good that even when a Fortune 500 OEM walked away, the product's engineering and the market's demand carried it forward. That's the strongest endorsement there is.

What Happened

1

The Installation Economics Gap

Tamarack's Transformation Centers complete a SMARTWING installation in approximately 250 hours, including paint. Textron's service centers — even after performing over 30 installations — required 750–1,000 hours per aircraft. At that labor rate, they were losing money on every install. When the economics didn't work for them, they began looking for a reason to step back.

2

November 2018 — The Catalyst

An accident involving a SMARTWING-equipped CJ in Indiana (aircraft was in compliance with all service bulletins) triggered an NTSB investigation. Separately, CJ3 fleet performance data created questions about whether certain aircraft were meeting expected cruise numbers. These events gave Textron the opening to distance themselves from the program.

3

2019 — Textron Walks Away

Textron Aviation stopped supporting Tamarack-modified aircraft — including 29 of the roughly 200 installations that Textron's own service centers had performed. The Product Support Agreement effectively ended. Tamarack became, in Textron's words, persona non grata.

4

The Grounding & the Comeback

EASA issued an emergency AD grounding the fleet in April 2019. The FAA followed under political pressure. Within 77 days, EASA lifted the grounding — citing existing service bulletins as sufficient. The FAA followed within days. By July 22, 2019, Tamarack resumed installations. The NTSB's final report (November 2022) found no design deficiency, and in February 2024, the NTSB granted Tamarack's petition for reconsideration — a rare reversal.

5

CJP — A Missed Opportunity for Advocacy

The Citation Jet Pilots Owner Association (CJP), which had financial ties to Textron, did not advocate for the SMARTWING fleet during the grounding or the NTSB process — even though hundreds of their own members were affected. This was a reminder that industry organizations sometimes have conflicting priorities.

6

Tamarack's Strategic Pivot

Rather than remain dependent on any single OEM relationship, Tamarack diversified: military contracts, airline programs, and expanding the Transformation Center network. The company emerged leaner, more self-sufficient, and with a product that had been publicly vindicated by both EASA and the NTSB.

7

2026 — Reconnecting with Textron

Tamarack has since earned certified improvements on the M2 platform. A meeting with Textron is scheduled for May 2026 to explore re-engagement. The engineering case is stronger than ever, the fleet has continued to grow, and the M2 improvements represent a clear value proposition for Textron's customer base.

  • Don't litigate CJP. If asked, say: "Industry associations have complex relationships with their sponsors. We focus on our customers directly."
  • Don't dwell on the grounding. Mention it only to show the resolution. The story is the comeback, not the crisis.
  • Confidence, not bitterness. If you're defensive, the prospect will smell it. Own the story.

Certification & Testing — What It Takes to Earn an STC

When a prospect asks "How do I know this is safe?" — you need to know exactly what Tamarack went through to earn this approval. This isn't hand-waving. This is years of engineering, testing, and regulatory scrutiny.

🔑 Why This Matters in Sales
Most prospects have no idea what goes into an STC. When you can walk them through the rigor — structural tests, flutter dives, icing flights, EMI testing, a full year of DO-160 lab work — you're not just selling a winglet. You're selling engineering credibility that exceeds what many OEM original certifications require.

The STC Standard

A Supplemental Type Certificate (STC) is the only legal way to sell aftermarket modifications for certified aircraft. The certification standards must meet or exceed those of the original aircraft certification. Tamarack's eco-SMARTWING was developed in collaboration with EASA, Cessna, Textron, and the FAA.

Design & Analysis

  • Estimated forces on the entire aircraft — wing, tail, fuselage, extension, winglet — across thousands of load cases
  • Variables: aircraft weights, fuel loads, speeds, altitudes, turbulence, gusts, high-G maneuvers
  • System failure analysis using Part 25 (airline) standards — because Part 23 had no regulations for this type of system
  • Failure scenarios: TACS full deflection (hard-over) at maximum speed, TACS jammed, oscillation analysis
  • Iterative design loop: multiple prototype variations analyzed, tested, and refined until all regulations were met

Structural Testing — The Numbers

Testing was performed on removed OEM wings with the SMARTWING modification installed, using large test fixtures with actuators and sensors. Certifying authorities witnessed all tests.

Carbon Fiber — Extra Rigor

  • Load Enhancement Factor (LEF): Carbon fiber loses up to 50% of strength when hot. Regulators require an additional 147% factor on top of normal requirements
  • Barely Visible Impact Damage (BVID): Before testing, carbon parts were struck with an 8 lb metal rod dropped from 3.11 feet at worst-case locations — simulating real-world damage a pilot might not see on preflight
  • Parts then had to pass limit and ultimate load testing with the impact damage and thermal factor applied

Flutter Testing

  • Ground Vibration Tests (GVT): Aircraft covered in accelerometers and deflection sensors, shaken in multiple modes to find any tendency for vibrations to grow
  • Flight testing: Strict "build-up" approach — speed increased by only 20 knots per flight while shaking the aircraft with TACS to try to induce flutter
  • Grand finale: CJ3 dived from FL450 at M.785, near full throttle, both pilots pushing forward, while vibrating the wing with TACS
  • Result: Aircraft proven flutter-free to 1.4 × MMO (40% faster than maximum operating speed)
💬 Sales Talking Point
"We dived a CJ3 from FL450 at Mach .785 while deliberately trying to shake the wings apart. The airplane was perfectly stable. Flutter-free to 40% above max operating speed."

Flight Characteristics & Failure Testing

  • Stalls, landings, takeoffs, single-engine maneuvers, rolls, controllability, crosswind landings — all with and without failure conditions
  • Successfully tested "one winglet removed" scenario — simulating bird strike, lightning strike, or any abnormal event
  • 4.5°/sec roll rate established and accepted by authorities as manageable even for pilots of normal skill
  • Two 16-year-old student pilots with no jet time and no upset training successfully performed recoveries — demonstrating the system's inherent safety

Icing Certification

  • Aircraft outfitted with foam/fiberglass ice shapes simulating worst-case 45-minute holding pattern in severe moisture
  • Ice shapes on wing, horizontal tail, vertical tail, extension, and winglets
  • Asymmetric testing: ice shapes on only one winglet
  • Aircraft had to demonstrate good flight characteristics, climb performance, and stall behavior with ice shapes installed

EMI & DO-160 Environmental Testing

  • Components tested to DO-160: temperature, humidity, electrical shocks, lightning strikes, electromagnetic/radio frequency interference, vibration
  • Actuators, control units, and wire harnesses all tested while performing their duties under environmental stress
  • Testing took over a year in a specialized laboratory in Wichita, Kansas
  • Endurance testing simulated over 1,000,000 flight hours to identify control system vulnerabilities

Fatigue Analysis

  • Extensive analysis validated by test to ensure the modification does not affect the fatigue life of the aircraft
  • Metal structures vibrate in flight — the amount of fatigue must be known and estimated for the full service life
  • Seven distinct load cases developed covering baseline and ATLAS-modified configurations

Post-STC: PMA & Continued Airworthiness

  • Parts Manufacture Approval (PMA): Separate FAA approval to manufacture and sell the STC — quality control, traceability from every part back to raw material, annual FAA audits
  • Part 145 Repair Station: Tamarack and its Transformation Centers are FAA-approved repair stations for installation
  • Fleet monitoring: FAA and Tamarack jointly review fleet data. Issues trigger service bulletins or Airworthiness Directives
  • Fleet today: 185+ aircraft, 81,000+ flight hours on the SMARTWING STC

SPIN Selling Framework Week 2

Session 6 — Instructor-led, 120 minutes. All ATLAS sales conversations use this methodology.

S — Situation

Establish the prospect's current aircraft, usage, mission profile

  • "What model are you flying?"
  • "How many hours per year?"
  • "What's your typical mission — distance, passenger count?"

P — Problem

Surface pain points the prospect may not have articulated

  • "Have you ever had to make a fuel stop you didn't want to?"
  • "Do you find the aircraft limiting at high-altitude airports?"
  • "What does your fuel bill look like annually?"

I — Implication

Deepen the pain, make the cost of inaction tangible

  • "What does that extra fuel stop cost you — in time, in fuel, in wear?"
  • "How does that limit where you can take the aircraft?"
  • "What's the opportunity cost when you can't make a nonstop trip?"

N — Need-Payoff

Let the prospect articulate the value of the solution

  • "If you could eliminate that fuel stop, what would that mean for your schedule?"
  • "What would an extra 300 nm of range open up for you?"
  • "If the winglets paid for themselves in under two years, would that change the conversation?"

Sales Probing Questions Week 2

A comprehensive library of probing questions to deepen discovery conversations. Use these alongside the SPIN framework to uncover needs, quantify pain, and move prospects to action.

Opening & Discovery Questions

Start here to establish context and invite the prospect to share their situation.

  • "How can we help?"
  • "Could you please give me some background to this?"
  • "Can you tell me more about the present situation/problem?"
  • "Tell me more about it."
  • "How long have you been thinking about this?"
  • "Why do you think it is happening?"
  • "What goals and objectives do you have for this?"
  • "What is your biggest challenge with this?"
  • "What are your key objectives with this?"
  • "What do you like about your current supplier?"
  • "What are you using/doing now?"
  • "Do you have any preference with regards to the solution?"
  • "What three key outcomes do you want from this?"
  • "Can you give me an example?"
  • "Can you be more specific?"
  • "How does this look/sound/feel to you?"
  • "Why are you seeking to do this work/project/engagement?"

Identifying Symptoms & Big-Picture Problems

Designed to uncover barriers, understand severity, and get to the root cause. These connect directly to the Problem and Implication stages of SPIN.

  • "Why isn't this particular service/product/situation working for you right now?"
  • "How long has it been an issue/problem?"
  • "Why do you think the issue/problem has been going on for so long?"
  • "How much longer can you afford to have the problem go unresolved?"
  • "How is it impacting your organization/customers/staff?"
  • "How severe is the problem?"
  • "When do you need the issue/problem fixed by?"
  • "Why have you been dealing with this for so long?"
  • "What bothers you the most about this situation/issue/problem?"
  • "What has prevented you from fixing this in the past?"
  • "What kind of timeframe are you working in to fix this?"
  • "Is this problem causing other problems?"
  • "Does your competition have these problems?"
  • "What is the biggest problem that you are facing with this?"
  • "What alternatives have you considered?"
  • "What are the intangible effects of the problem?"
  • "Does the issue cause problems with employee morale?"
  • "Can this problem affect productivity?"
  • "Is this problem unique to your organization?"
  • "Is this an industry-wide problem?"
  • "When you went to your existing supplier and shared your frustrations about this problem, what reassurances did they give you?"
  • "How did these problems first come about? What were the original causes?"
  • "What have you done in the past to address the problem?"
  • "What kind of pressure is this causing you and the business?"
  • "What options have you tried?"
  • "What are the long-term effects of the problem?"
  • "How does the problem ultimately affect your current customers?"
  • "How does the problem ultimately affect your sales process?"
  • "How does the problem ultimately affect your reputation/goodwill/brand?"
  • "Do you feel this problem has given your competition a competitive advantage? If so, how?"
  • "How often do you think the problem has come up when you weren't aware of it?"
  • "If you were in your competitors' shoes, how would you take advantage of this?"

Action-Oriented Questions

Once you understand the barriers, help your prospect see the pathway forward. These map to Need-Payoff in SPIN — they build urgency and establish trust.

  • "What number would you put on this issue in terms of prioritization?"
  • "How much more productive could your people be if the problem did not exist?"
  • "If you were your competition, what would you do right now?"
  • "If you could design the perfect solution, what would it look like, how much would you spend, and how long would it go for?"
  • "What sense of urgency do you have here?"
  • "What three key outcomes do you want from solving the problem?"
  • "What are your top three requirements that this solution just has to have?"
  • "If you could have things the way you wanted, what would it look like?"
  • "How important is this need (on a scale of 1–10)?"
  • "What options are you currently looking at?"
  • "In a perfect world, what would you like to see happen with this?"
  • "What is your strategy to fix this problem?"
  • "What are you currently doing to address the problem?"

Financial Probing Questions

Revenue drives decisions. Transition into these once trust is established — they quantify the cost of inaction and frame your solution as an investment, not an expense.

  • "What is it costing you?"
  • "Do you know in what other areas the problem is costing you money?"
  • "Can you put an amount on the problem in terms of cost: weekly, monthly, annually?"
  • "Can you see how much money you lose every day by not solving this issue?"
  • "How does the problem ultimately affect your pricing/selling costs?"
  • "How much does this problem cost you in man hours/time?"
  • "Looking at this from a point of lost sales, how much is just one sale worth to the company?"
  • "How much is the issue costing you in time/money/resources/staff/energy?"
  • "Can you make an educated guess as to how much it costs you?"
  • "What kind of return or payoff will you be looking for if you get a successful resolution?"
  • "What are you working with at the moment? Just a ballpark..."
  • "How do you handle budget considerations?"

Accountability & Clarity Questions

Wrap up your discovery by confirming scope, decision-makers, and next steps. These solidify understanding and ensure nothing is missed.

  • "Who is ultimately responsible for this?"
  • "Who else is aware of it?"
  • "What has made you want to look into this now?"
  • "What kind of timeframe are you working within?"
  • "Is there anything I have overlooked?"
  • "Have I covered everything?"
  • "What alternatives have you considered?"
  • "Do you have any questions you'd like to ask me?"
  • "What other factors have we not discussed that are important to you?"
  • "Are there any other areas I haven't asked you about that are important?"
  • "What else should I know?"
  • "Have I asked you about every detail that's important to you?"
  • "How soon would you like to move with this?"
  • "What's your role in this situation/issue/problem?"
  • "Who supports this action?"
💡 Pro Tip You don't ask all of these in one call. Pick 3–5 from each category that feel natural for the persona and situation. The goal is to listen more than you talk — ask, then let the prospect fill the silence.

Buyer Personas Week 2

Session 7 — Know your persona within the first 2 minutes of the call. It determines everything else.

The Go-Getter

Self-made, 45–65, time is money. Competitive edge, status.

Lead with: Range/speed gains and business case. Be prepared and direct — don't waste their time.

The Collector

Affluent, multiple aircraft, values craftsmanship & exclusivity.

Lead with: Vref valuation, aesthetics, engineering pedigree. They buy the best — position ATLAS as the best.

The Old Pro

Career aviator, 55+, thousands of hours. Respects experience & safety.

Lead with: Technical depth, test data, pilot testimonials. Don't oversell — let the data speak.

The Skeptic

Analytical, research-driven. Control, certainty, risk mitigation.

Lead with: STC documentation, AD transparency, demo flight. Proactively address history — never dodge questions.

Hard Conversations — AD History & Chapter 11 Week 2

Session 10 — CRITICAL SESSION. This separates good reps from great ones.

⚠️ Every serious prospect will ask about these. You must be prepared.

The Airworthiness Directive (AD)

  1. What happened: Factual, concise timeline
  2. What was done: Engineering response, testing, resolution
  3. Current status: Full compliance, no outstanding issues
  4. Key message: "The AD process is exactly how aviation safety is supposed to work. A concern was identified, it was addressed with engineering rigor, and the aircraft returned to service safer than before."

Chapter 11 / Company History

Be transparent — don't dodge it. Frame: "The company reorganized, retained its engineering team, and emerged with the same product and stronger support infrastructure."

Anatomy of the Cold Call Week 3

Session 11 — Break down the call into a repeatable structure.

Call Structure

  1. Opening (10 sec) — Name, company, one-sentence hook:
    "Hi [Name], this is [Rep] with Tamarack Aerospace. We make the ATLAS Active Winglets for the [model] — I wanted to share some performance data that's relevant to your aircraft."
  2. Permission (5 sec) — "Do you have 2 minutes, or is there a better time?"
  3. SPIN sequence (2–3 min) — Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff
  4. Bridge to next step (30 sec) — Close on the meeting, not on the phone:
    "I'd love to send you the spec comparison for your specific model and set up a 15-minute call to walk through it."

Voicemail Protocol (30 seconds max)

"Hi [Name], [Rep] with Tamarack. I have some range and fuel data specific to your [model] that I think you'd find interesting. My number is [xxx]. Again, this is [Rep] with Tamarack."

Closing Techniques Week 4

Session 18 — Closing isn't a trick — it's the natural conclusion of a well-run process.

The Summary Close

Restate their pain, your solution, and the ROI. "Based on everything we've discussed — the fuel savings, the range improvement, the resale value — does it make sense to schedule the installation?"

The Assumptive Close

When buying signals are strong. "Let's look at the installation calendar. What quarter works best for your schedule?"

The Puppy Dog Close

For hesitant prospects. "Why don't we schedule a demo flight so you can feel the difference yourself? No commitment, just data."

The Takeaway

For stalled deals. "In the meantime, you'll fly about [X] hours and burn about [Y] more gallons than you would with ATLAS. Just something to think about."
