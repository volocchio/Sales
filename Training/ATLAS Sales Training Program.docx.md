# ATLAS Active Winglet — Sales Training Program

## Program Overview

**Duration:** 6 weeks (30 sessions)
**Format:** 5 sessions per week — mix of classroom instruction, role-play, ride-along, and self-study
**Audience:** New sales team members with no prior ATLAS product knowledge
**Objective:** Deliver a sales professional who can independently prospect, qualify, pitch, handle objections, close, and support post-sale follow-through across all Citation and M2 platforms.

#### Session 2: Aerodynamic Principles — Why Winglets Work
**Instructor-led, 90 minutes**

Teach from first principles so the rep can explain the product to a skeptical pilot:

1. **Lift-induced drag** — spanwise airflow, wingtip vortices, energy loss
2. **Aspect ratio** — why a longer, narrower wing is more efficient
3. **Passive vs. active winglets** — passive winglets add span but also add structural load and drag in turbulence; active winglets use the ATLAS load-alleviation system to allow 2–3x more span extension without structural penalty
4. **The ATLAS system** — active load alleviation via accelerometers, control module, and TACS (Tamarack Active Camber Surface) on each winglet; responds in milliseconds to gusts; certified under FAA STC
5. **Analogy toolkit:** "Think of passive winglets as wearing a heavy backpack to hike farther. ATLAS gives you the same distance with a lighter pack — because it actively manages the weight distribution in real time."

**Key numbers to memorize:**
- Active winglets are 2–3x more efficient than passive winglets
- ATLAS-equipped aircraft see 15–25% fuel burn reduction depending on model
- Over 220 SMARTWING™ installations completed worldwide
- Over 1 billion gallons of fuel saved industry-wide by winglet technology

**Assessment:** Whiteboard exercise — draw and explain lift-induced drag and how ATLAS addresses it

### Week 2 — Sales Methodology & Customer Psychology

#### Session 6: The SPIN Selling Framework
**Instructor-led, 120 minutes**

All ATLAS sales scripts use the **SPIN** methodology. Teach the full framework:

1. **Situation Questions** — establish the prospect's current aircraft, usage, mission profile
   - "What model are you flying?"
   - "How many hours per year?"
   - "What's your typical mission — distance, passenger count?"

2. **Problem Questions** — surface pain points the prospect may not have articulated
   - "Have you ever had to make a fuel stop you didn't want to?"
   - "Do you find the aircraft limiting at high-altitude airports?"
   - "What does your fuel bill look like annually?"

3. **Implication Questions** — deepen the pain, make the cost of inaction tangible
   - "What does that extra fuel stop cost you — in time, in fuel, in wear?"
   - "How does that limit where you can take the aircraft?"
   - "What's the opportunity cost when you can't make a nonstop trip?"

4. **Need-Payoff Questions** — let the prospect articulate the value of the solution
   - "If you could eliminate that fuel stop, what would that mean for your schedule?"
   - "What would an extra 300 nm of range open up for you?"
   - "If the winglets paid for themselves in under two years, would that change the conversation?"

**Practice:** Pairs role-play — one person plays a CJ2 owner who flies 300 hours/year, the other runs the SPIN sequence. Rotate.

**Reference materials:** `ATLAS Sales Kit West Region 2.docx`, `ATLAS Sales Kit LongTerm Owner 2.docx`

#### Session 7: Buyer Personas
**Instructor-led, 60 minutes + workshop**

Introduce the four buyer personas. For each, cover demographics, emotional needs, rational needs, and how to adjust approach:

**The Go-Getter**
- Demographics: Self-made, 45–65, time is money
- Emotional need: Competitive edge, status
- Rational need: Performance data, ROI
- Approach: Lead with range/speed gains and business case. Don't waste their time — be prepared and direct.

**The Collector**
- Demographics: Affluent, multiple aircraft, values craftsmanship
- Emotional need: Exclusivity, aesthetic appeal
- Rational need: Resale value, quality of installation
- Approach: Lead with Vref valuation, aesthetics, and the engineering pedigree. They buy the best — position ATLAS as the best.

**The Old Pro**
- Demographics: Career aviator, 55+, thousands of hours
- Emotional need: Respect for experience, safety
- Rational need: Technical proof, test data, pilot testimonials
- Approach: Go deep on aerodynamics. Share pilot feedback. Don't oversell — let the data speak. They'll respect you for knowing your stuff.

**The Skeptic**
- Demographics: Analytical, research-driven, will find every forum post
- Emotional need: Control, certainty, risk mitigation
- Rational need: STC documentation, test flight data, AD history
- Approach: Proactively address the AD history (Session 10). Provide documentation unprompted. Invite them to a demo flight. The worst thing you can do is dodge their questions.

**Workshop:** Given a brief prospect description, identify the persona and outline your first call approach. 4 scenarios, 15 minutes each.

**Reference materials:** `The Collector.pdf`, `The Go-Getter.pdf`, `The Old Pro.pdf`, `The Skeptic.pdf`

#### Session 8: ROI — Building the Business Case
**Instructor-led, 90 minutes**

The three-step ROI framework:

**Step 1: Hard Asset Value**
- Vref (industry blue book) assigns 100% ROI at installation, 90% at year 2
- This means the modification pays for itself in aircraft resale value alone
- "This is the only mod I know of where you get your money back before you even fly it."

**Step 2: Soft Value Assessment**
Rate each category 1–5 and multiply:
1. Buying habits / comps in market
2. Aesthetics
3. Safety
4. Comfort / convenience
5. Dispatch robustness
6. Environmental impact
7. Range improvement
8. Payload improvement

A combined soft value of 20–30% above the install price is typical.

**Step 3: Fuel Savings**
Real-world data calculation:
- Example: CJ2 owner flying 300 hrs/year
- Stock burn: 900 pph × 300 hr = 270,000 lbs fuel/year
- ATLAS burn: 690 pph × 300 hr = 207,000 lbs fuel/year
- Savings: 63,000 lbs × $5.50/gal ÷ 6.7 lbs/gal = **~$51,800/year**
- At $199K install price → **payback in under 4 years from fuel alone** (ignoring Vref)

**Practice:** Each rep builds a custom ROI for a provided prospect profile. Present to the group.

**Reference materials:** `ROI Narrative.pdf`, `ROI Rev A.pdf`, `Smarty pants ROI what does it really mean.pdf`

#### Session 9: Customer Testimonials & Flight Stories
**Self-study + discussion, 60 minutes**

Read and internalize real customer experiences:
- CJ owner who flew 1,853 nm nonstop (world record)
- CJ3 Shannon-to-Gander transatlantic crossing

- Mosier world trip in CJ3
- Air Charter Scotland 135 operation with ATLAS CJ1
- Multiple FlightAware track screenshots showing real-world long-range flights

**Key principle:** "Stories sell. Data supports. Lead with a story that matches the prospect's mission, then back it up with the numbers."

**Build your story bank:** Each rep selects 2 stories per model that resonate with them and practices telling them in under 90 seconds.

**Reference materials:** `Atlas experience-customer's experience.docx`, `Pilot feedback and experiences.docx`, `525B Flight stories/` folder

#### Session 10: Handling the Hard Conversations — AD History & Chapter 11
**Instructor-led, 90 minutes (CRITICAL SESSION)**

This is the session that separates good reps from great ones. Every serious prospect will ask about:

1. **The Airworthiness Directive (AD) history**
   - What happened: factual, concise timeline
   - What was done: engineering response, testing, resolution
   - Current status: full compliance, no outstanding issues
   - Key message: "The AD process is exactly how aviation safety is supposed to work. A concern was identified, it was addressed with engineering rigor, and the aircraft returned to service safer than before."

2. **Chapter 11 / Company history**
   - Be transparent — don't dodge it
   - Frame it: "The company reorganized, retained its engineering team, and emerged with the same product and stronger support infrastructure."

3. **Prospect objection patterns:**
   - "I heard they were grounded" → Acknowledge, explain resolution, pivot to current fleet performance
   - "Is the company stable?" → Point to active fleet size, ongoing STC work, customer retention
   - "My mechanic says..." → Offer to connect their mechanic with Tamarack engineering directly

**Role-play:** 3 rounds. Instructor plays a hostile Skeptic persona asking the hardest questions. Rep must stay calm, factual, and confident.

**Reference materials:** `AD Talking Points.pdf`, `Diane White Response_bk edits.docx`, `FAQ.docx`

## PHASE 2: EXECUTION (Week 3–4)

### Week 3 — Call Craft & Prospecting

#### Session 11: Anatomy of the Cold Call
**Instructor-led + live demonstration, 90 minutes**

Break down the cold call structure:

1. **Opening (10 seconds)** — Name, company, one-sentence hook
   - "Hi [Name], this is [Rep] with Tamarack Aerospace. We make the ATLAS Active Winglets for the [model] — I wanted to share some performance data that's relevant to your aircraft."

2. **Permission (5 seconds)** — Respect their time
   - "Do you have 2 minutes, or is there a better time?"

3. **SPIN sequence (2–3 minutes)** — Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff

4. **Bridge to next step (30 seconds)** — Don't close on the phone; close on the meeting
   - "I'd love to send you the spec comparison for your specific model and set up a 15-minute call to walk through it. What day works?"

5. **Voicemail protocol** — You will leave a lot of voicemails. Make them count:
   - 30 seconds max
   - Name, company, one benefit, callback number, repeat name
   - "Hi [Name], [Rep] with Tamarack. I have some range and fuel data specific to your [model] that I think you'd find interesting. My number is [xxx]. Again, this is [Rep] with Tamarack."

**Reference materials:** `Cold Call Script-Limpkin.docx`, `ATLAS Callback Script (1) 1.docx`

#### Session 12: Regional & Owner-Type Prospecting
**Instructor-led, 90 minutes**

Two distinct prospect types require different approaches:

**New Owner (< 2 years with aircraft)**
- Motivation: Still investing in the aircraft, open to upgrades
- Approach: "You just made a significant investment. ATLAS protects and enhances that investment — Vref assigns 100% value from day one."
- Timing: Reach them within first 6 months of ownership if possible

**Long-Term Owner (5+ years)**
- Motivation: Knows the aircraft's limitations intimately, may be considering selling
- Approach: "You know this aircraft better than anyone. You've felt the limitations at altitude, on hot days. ATLAS addresses every one of those. And if you're ever thinking of selling, it increases your resale value."
- Additional objections unique to long-term owners:
  - "I've flown this long without winglets" → "And imagine how much fuel and time you could have saved. The best time to install was 5 years ago. The second best time is now."
  - "I'm thinking of upgrading to a bigger aircraft" → "ATLAS gives you bigger-aircraft performance at a fraction of the cost. A $199K mod vs. a $2M trade-up."

**Regional considerations:** Know the airports, weather patterns, and mission profiles typical in your territory.

**Reference materials:** `ATLAS Sales Kit West Region 2.docx`, `ATLAS Sales Kit LongTerm Owner 2.docx`

#### Session 13: Objection Handling Masterclass
**Workshop, 120 minutes**

The 13 standard objections and how to handle each:

| # | Objection | Response Framework |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | "Too expensive" | ROI framework — Vref + fuel savings = payback in 1–2 years |
| 2 | "I don't fly enough to justify it" | Even at 100 hrs/year, Vref alone justifies it. Plus safety and performance gains aren't usage-dependent |
| 3 | "I'm selling the aircraft" | ATLAS increases resale value — Vref data proves it. You'll get more than you paid for the mod |
| 4 | "My mechanic/pilot doesn't like it" | Offer direct conversation with Tamarack engineering. Share pilot testimonials |
| 5 | "I heard about the AD" | Session 10 framework — acknowledge, explain, pivot to current performance |
| 6 | "I need to think about it" | "Absolutely. What specific questions can I answer to help you think it through?" |
| 7 | "Send me information" | "Happy to. What's the one thing you'd most want to see — performance data, ROI, or pilot feedback?" Then follow up in 3 days |
| 8 | "I already looked into it" | "When was that? A lot has changed. Can I show you the current data?" |
| 9 | "What about the company's financial stability?" | Transparent answer — reorganized, engineering team intact, growing fleet |
| 10 | "I'll wait for the next model" | "The mod is available now and Vref gives you full value. Waiting costs fuel every flight" |
| 11 | "My aircraft isn't worth modifying" | "Vref disagrees — the mod adds measurable value. And the performance gains transform the mission capability" |
| 12 | "I fly short legs, don't need range" | "Range is just one benefit. The fuel savings, climb performance, and payload gains apply to every flight, short or long" |

**Drill:** Rapid-fire — instructor throws objections, rep has 15 seconds to respond. 3 rounds of all 13.

### Week 4 — Demo Flights & Closing

#### Session 16: Demo Flight Preparation & Execution
**Instructor-led + field observation, full day**

The demo flight is the most powerful tool in the sales process. Structure it properly:

**Pre-flight:**
- Confirm prospect's typical mission (airports, distances, passenger count)
- Plan a route that demonstrates the key advantages for their model
- Brief the demo pilot on the prospect's persona and hot-button issues
- Have printed spec sheet and ROI summary ready for post-flight

**During flight:**
- Let the prospect fly if rated and they want to
- Point out: climb rate, fuel flow, ride quality, stability
- Don't oversell during the flight — let the aircraft speak
- Note the prospect's reactions for post-flight discussion

**Post-flight debrief:**
- "What stood out to you?"
- "How does that compare to what you're used to?"
- Bridge to proposal: "Based on what you saw today, let me put together the numbers for your specific situation."

**Reference materials:** `Proper Demo Flight with tips and tricks for CJ.docx`, `CJ3 Q&A Dan Caroll.docx`

#### Session 17: Building & Presenting Proposals
**Instructor-led, 90 minutes**

- Customize the proposal to the prospect's stated needs (from SPIN discovery)
- Include: spec comparison for their model, custom ROI calculation, 2–3 relevant customer stories, installation timeline
- Present in person or video call — never just email a PDF
- Walk through each section; let them ask questions
- End with a clear ask: "Are you ready to move forward, or is there something else you need?"

#### Session 18: Closing Techniques
**Workshop, 90 minutes**

Closing isn't a trick — it's the natural conclusion of a well-run process:

1. **The Summary Close** — Restate their pain, your solution, and the ROI. "Based on everything we've discussed — the fuel savings, the range improvement, the resale value — does it make sense to schedule the installation?"

2. **The Assumptive Close** — When buying signals are strong. "Let's look at the installation calendar. What quarter works best for your schedule?"

3. **The Puppy Dog Close** — For hesitant prospects. "Why don't we schedule a demo flight so you can feel the difference yourself? No commitment, just data."

4. **The Takeaway** — For stalled deals. "I understand the timing isn't right. I'll check back in 90 days. In the meantime, you'll fly about [X] hours and burn about [Y] more gallons than you would with ATLAS. Just something to think about."

**Role-play:** Full sales cycle — from cold call to close — in 20-minute compressed scenarios. 3 rounds with different personas.

#### Session 19: Post-Sale & Referral Generation
**Instructor-led, 60 minutes**

- Installation handoff process
- Check-in cadence: 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, 6 months post-install
- Collecting testimonials and FlightAware screenshots from satisfied customers
- Referral ask: "Who else in your hangar / flying club / ownership group should know about this?"
- Turning customers into advocates

2. Makes the cold call (role-play with instructor)
3. Handles objections
4. Presents a tailored ROI
5. Conducts a post-demo debrief
6. Attempts to close
